**Short answer:** In most cases, **no**. Under Australian federal and state anti-discrimination laws, a landlord or resident provider generally **cannot refuse you, screen you out, or treat you less favourably because you are an international student**, because of your nationality, ethnicity, or where you were born. They *can* apply lawful, practical tenancy criteria (affordability, house rules, lease length, references) as long as those criteria are applied fairly to everyone.

## What Australian law says

Australia has a layered set of laws that protect people from discrimination when accessing accommodation.

At the federal level, the **Racial Discrimination Act 1975 (Cth)** makes it unlawful to treat someone unfavourably because of race, colour, descent, national or ethnic origin, or immigrant status in many areas of public life, including accommodation.

State and territory laws add further protections. Depending on where the property is, you may also be covered by laws such as:

- **NSW** — *Anti-Discrimination Act 1977*
- **VIC** — *Equal Opportunity Act 2010*
- **QLD** — *Anti-Discrimination Act 1991*
- **WA** — *Equal Opportunity Act 1984*
- **SA** — *Equal Opportunity Act 1984*
- **ACT** — *Discrimination Act 1991*
- **TAS** — *Anti-Discrimination Act 1998*
- **NT** — *Anti-Discrimination Act 1992*

These laws commonly protect attributes including nationality, race, religion, sex, disability, age, and family responsibilities. The exact wording and exemptions vary by state, but the core principle is consistent: **you should not be excluded from housing because you are an international student.**

## Direct and indirect discrimination

**Direct discrimination** is straightforward: a landlord refuses your application, ignores your messages, or publishes a listing that says "no international students" or "Australians only" because of your background or visa status.

**Indirect discrimination** is more subtle. A rule that looks neutral on its face can still be unlawful if it disproportionately disadvantages a protected group and is not reasonable. For example, requiring a guarantor or six months of Australian rental history in a way that effectively screens out most new arrivals *because of their migrant or student status* may raise discrimination concerns, depending on how it is applied.

## What landlords can lawfully consider

Landlords and resident providers are not required to accept every applicant. They may assess whether a tenancy is likely to work in practice, using criteria that relate to the property and the household, such as:

- Whether the advertised rent is affordable for the applicant
- Whether the applicant agrees to reasonable house rules (for example, no smoking indoors)
- Lease dates and minimum stay that suit the room or property
- References, rental history, or evidence of income or support where requested consistently of all applicants
- Compatibility with an existing household in a shared home (noise, study needs, shared spaces), assessed without stereotyping

What they **cannot** do is use those assessments as a cover for excluding people on the basis of nationality, race, religion, or other protected attributes.

## Shared homes and live-in landlords

Some anti-discrimination laws include **narrow exceptions** for accommodation in which the owner or a resident provider lives on the premises and shares living space. The scope of those exceptions differs by state and is often limited to who the resident provider chooses to share their own home with.

Even where a narrow exception might apply, that does **not** make discriminatory advertising acceptable. Public listings should not use exclusionary language targeting international students or any other protected group. On Quni, our Non-Discrimination Policy applies regardless of living arrangement.

## What to do if you are refused unfairly

If you believe a landlord or listing has discriminated against you:

1. **Keep records** — save the listing text, messages, and dates.
2. **Report it on the platform** — on Quni, contact [hello@quni.com.au](mailto:hello@quni.com.au) so we can review listings and accounts under our Non-Discrimination Policy.
3. **Seek external advice** — you can contact the **Australian Human Rights Commission** for federal complaints, or your state or territory equal opportunity commission. Many offer free information services.
4. **Keep looking** — many landlords welcome international students; verified marketplaces that enforce fair housing rules can reduce the risk of discriminatory screening.

## Key takeaways

- **Refusing accommodation because someone is an international student is usually unlawful** under Australian anti-discrimination law.
- **Legitimate tenancy criteria are fine** when applied consistently and for real housing reasons, not as a proxy for discrimination.
- **Listings and messages matter** — "no international students" wording is a red flag for both renters and regulators.
- **You have options** — platform reporting, human rights bodies, and fair-housing marketplaces can help you find a lawful, respectful place to live.
